Goal Attainment Scaling (GAS)

While there are many ways to use the results of the Checklists, one important strategy is to collaborate with the individual with developmental disabilities to create goals and measure success. One method is to use Goal Attainment Scaling (Kiresuk & Sherman, 1968)1 or GAS.

What is Goal Attainment Scaling?

Goal Attainment Scaling (GAS) is a personalized method for setting and evaluating progress toward specific objectives. Unlike traditional goal-setting techniques, GAS allows users to define their own baseline and levels of achievement, ensuring that progress is meaningful and individualized. The approach is particularly useful in healthcare, caregiving, research, and personal development.

How Does It Work?

Users start by identifying a target goal and setting a range of outcomes from -2 (significantly below expectation) to +2 (exceeding expectation). This scaling system provides a clear way to assess improvement, challenges, and successes over time.

Why Use GAS?

  • Structured yet flexible: Adapts to individual needs while maintaining measurable outcomes.

  • Encourages realistic progress: Helps break goals into manageable steps.

  • Enhances self-reflection: Facilitates insights into challenges and strengths.

Developing a GAS

A Likert scale is used to describe the levels of effectiveness of the interventions in using a goal attainment scaling, with a baseline. The Table below shows an general example.

Rating

Description

+2 - Greatly Exceeds Expectations

The individual has made substantial progress, significantly beyond the expected goal outcome (i.e.: +2 or higher on the GAS scale). The intervention has led to remarkable success in achieving and surpassing the goal.

+1 - Exceeds Expectations

The individual has performed beyond the expected goal outcome but not as much at the 'Greatly Exceeds' level (i.e.: between 0 and +1 on the GAS scale). The intervention has been effective in helping the individual achieve the goal and develop additional skills or behaviors.

0 - Goal Attained

The individual has met the expected goal outcome (i.e.: 0 on the GAS scale). The goal has been fully achieved, and the individual is able to consistently demonstrate the desired behavior or skill.

-1 - Baseline

The individual's current performance is aligned with their baseline, showing little or no progress towards the goal (i.e.: between baseline and expected outcome). Significant gaps remain between the baseline and the expected goal, indicating minimal progress or improvement.

-2 - Does Not Meet Expectations

The individual has regressed from the baseline, meaning the intervention has been largely ineffective, possibly due to factors such as lack of focus, illness, personal circumstances, or other barriers preventing progress. The individual is performing below their baseline.



Explanation of Adjustments:

  1. Clarification of GAS Terminology: The language around +1 and +2 has been refined to make it more consistent with the standard interpretation of GAS scales.

    • +2 ('Greatly Exceeds Expectations'): Clearly indicates a level of performance that goes significantly beyond the goal.

    • +1 ('Exceeds Expectations'): Indicates that the individual performed above expectations, but not to the extent of +2.

  2. Baseline as -1: In GAS, -1 is typically used to reflect performance that is below the expected outcome but still represents progress from the baseline. I've clarified this in the scale so that it reflects that the individual is still below expectations but is not at the baseline of no progress.

  3. Regression as -2: The -2 rating is defined as 'Does Not Meet Expectations,' which is a regression or lack of progress. I've retained the idea that this can happen due to factors outside the individual's control, like illness or external factors.

  4. Consistency with Goal Outcomes: The descriptions are now more clearly tied to the GAS scale's numeric outcomes (+2, +1, 0, -1, -2), so that each rating is grounded in clear criteria for assessment.

This scale will help track the individual's progress over time, comparing their baseline with the expected goal and showing how effectively the intervention is supporting progress.
